Toward agentic engineering at Wellhub

An analysis of the SPDD/cia proposal, and the path to AI-native delivery.

The current proposal is intellectually serious and well disciplined. It is, however, a governance instinct expressed in agentic language. It hardens the floor of AI usage at Wellhub; it does not yet raise the ceiling. The work ahead is to keep the floor, and build the ceiling.

This document reviews the proposal "Structured Prompt-Driven Development (SPDD) for BASE & CIA" and assesses how well it positions Wellhub for the shift toward agentic engineering. The intent is constructive. Most of what the proposal does well should be preserved. Several structural decisions, however, need to evolve before the design can support a genuinely AI-native operating model.

The analysis is organised in three movements. First, the elements of the current proposal that should be kept and built upon. Second, the gaps that limit its ability to deliver AI-native outcomes. Third, a concrete picture of what an AI-native version looks like, together with the recommended path to get there.

1.Strengths to preserve

The proposal demonstrates an unusual degree of discipline for an AI initiative. Several decisions are already aligned with how AI-native organisations operate, and they should survive any redesign.

Prompts treated as first-class artifacts

Versioning prompts, reviewing them like code, and keeping them in two-way sync with the codebase (via prompt-update and sync) is the single strongest idea in the document. Most enterprises adopting AI end up with private chat histories and a folklore-grade understanding of their own workflows. This proposal refuses that failure mode from page one.

Binding to the existing SDLC

Each cia command maps to a stage Wellhub already runs (RFC, Privacy/Fraud/Infosec sign-off, DB migrations, deploy windows, post-deploy monitoring, on-call runbooks). This avoids the most common failure pattern of internal AI initiatives, which is becoming a sandbox no one ships from.

A disciplined Non-Goals section

Explicitly excluding hotfixes, exploratory spikes, one-off scripts, and aesthetic work shows mature scoping. Refusing to bypass CODEOWNERS, Infosec, Privacy, and Fraud approvals reinforces the same instinct.

The Code Red audit story

Making every AI-assisted change traceable back to the prompt that produced it is exactly the answer Infosec and Privacy want to hear, and increasingly the answer regulators and auditors will want as well. This is a defensible, hard-to-copy outcome.

Norms and Safeguards as shared organisational assets

Pulling team conventions and non-negotiables out of individual prompts and into a governed catalog produces the right primitive. These behave like organisational "house rules" for any AI workflow, and they will outlive whatever tooling Wellhub adopts next.

Operationally sane technical choices

Single Go binary, model-agnostic, gateway-mediated calls, per-service .spdd.yaml, central templates pinned by version. Nothing exotic. It can be built, and it can be operated.

Headline for section 1. The strongest contribution of the proposal is its artifact discipline: prompts in git, reviewed like code, kept in sync with what shipped, sitting on top of a shared Norms and Safeguards catalog. Whatever Wellhub builds next, this should be kept.

2.Critical gaps in the current design

While the foundations are strong, several structural decisions limit the proposal's ability to deliver an AI-native operating model. The observations below are ordered by impact, with the most consequential first. They are intended as construction notes for the next iteration of the design, not as a verdict on the work done so far.

2.1 The design does not yet contain agents

The document is titled "SDLC - Agents," but architecturally it is a CLI that fills structured prompt templates and returns the LLM's response for human review. There is no plan, act, observe loop; no tool use inside an agent; no self-correction; no subagent delegation; no background workers; and no agent that initiates anything on its own. The word agent appears as a label, not as a design element. This is structured prompting, which is useful and disciplined, but it sits in a different capability category from agentic engineering.

2.2 AI is positioned as a generator, not a collaborator

The proposal assumes the engineer is always the author and the AI is called in moments to draft on demand. That assumption leaves most of today's productivity gains on the table. Modern AI-native delivery is continuous: in the editor (tools such as Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot Workspace), on the pull request (review agents, automated test generation), and in operations (on-call triage, autonomous alert tuning). A CLI invoked deliberately before doing work is the wrong surface area for any of those gains.

2.3 The bottleneck identified is not the bottleneck solved

The Abstract diagnoses the constraint correctly: delivery is limited by ambiguity, review load, inconsistency, and production risk, not by typing speed. The proposed remedy then adds artifacts that humans must produce and review, which shifts review load rather than reducing it. The real lever for the stated bottleneck is agentic pre-review, agentic test generation, and agentic incident triage, none of which appear in the design.

2.4 No evaluation, and therefore no learning loop

Versioning a prompt does not mean improving it. The proposal does not mention golden eval sets, regression CI for prompt changes, quality dashboards, or feedback signals (PR rework rate, review comments per AI-generated artifact, escape defects) flowing back into the prompt library. Without this, the "compounding team asset" claim has no mechanism behind it. Prompts will drift and decay in the same way most internal documentation does. Evaluation is the single highest-leverage capability missing from the current design.

2.5 No tool layer for agents (MCP or equivalent)

Integrations with Datadog, Argo, Vault, Sonarcloud, the gympass-app catalog, the Production Queries sheet, and IAM are all delivered through bespoke scripts inside a single binary. The industry is converging on the Model Context Protocol (MCP) as the standard way to expose internal systems to AI agents. Building a parallel script-orchestration layer creates two problems at once: it produces technical debt the day it ships, and it traps these capabilities inside the CLI. No IDE agent, PR-review agent, or on-call agent could call them without being rewritten.

2.6 No autonomy gradient

Every command sits at the same maturity level: human invokes, AI drafts, human approves. A serious agentic strategy distinguishes between copilot (suggests), autopilot (acts with confirmation), and autonomous (acts within guardrails), and explicitly moves specific workflows along that gradient over time. Without this framing, nothing in the design can ever become more than a copilot, which is precisely where most enterprise AI programmes stall.

2.7 The security model needs more depth

The phrase "the gateway redacts what needs redacting" is a sentence, not a design. For an organisation operating under GDPR, LGPD, and PCI scope, this needs explicit answers on PII and secrets detection, model-provider data-handling SLAs, prompt and response retention, prompt-injection defence (which becomes load-bearing the moment agents act rather than only suggest), and blast-radius limits for agents holding credentials. None of these are addressed at the depth the rest of the document deserves.

2.8 Hotfixes, spikes, and exploratory work are explicitly excluded

The exclusion is intellectually honest, but operationally significant. In any real engineering organisation those modes account for a large share of weekly throughput. Engineers will continue to use their IDE and an AI chat for that work. SPDD will therefore be reserved for "important" changes, which is exactly the failure pattern of every governance tool that did not embed itself in the daily flow.

2.9 Several commands are designed as scripts where they should be agents

Reading the command catalog carefully, several entries are natural candidates for autonomous loops rather than human-invoked scripts:

  • cia post-deploy queries Argo, Datadog, and Grafana for the one-hour window after every deploy.
  • cia alert-coverage diffs current alerts against the canonical catalog and opens PRs.
  • cia deploy-window enforces a time-of-day rule.
  • cia db-query-plan compares execution plans before and after.

These are bounded, low-blast-radius workflows that should run continuously, not scripts a human must remember to invoke. This is the clearest place where the design leaves the AI-native opportunity unrealised, and one of the easiest places to demonstrate value early.

2.10 Adoption, cost, and outcomes are unspecified

No pilot scope, no rollout sequence, no deprecation plan for competing workflows, no projected token spend or ROI threshold, and no commitment to which numbers will move. Cycle time, change failure rate, MTTR, review turnaround, incidents per thousand changes: none are named as success metrics. This is the most addressable of the gaps, and the one that will most affect leadership confidence in the programme.

3.Positioning: AI-assisted versus AI-native

Three operating postures are routinely conflated in internal pitches. Separating them clarifies the conversation about where this proposal lands.

Posture What humans do What AI does Where the proposal sits
AI-assisted Author everything (code, docs, PRs, reviews). Invoke AI in moments, on demand. Drafts, suggests, autocompletes, always inside a human-driven loop. Here
AI-augmented Set intent, review diffs, approve. AI runs in the editor and in CI continuously. Generates whole diffs, runs reviews, fixes lints, drafts RFCs, files alerts. Humans steer per task. Adjacent
AI-native Set direction, define guardrails, approve high-stakes actions, handle escalations. Operates as a teammate: opens PRs, triages alerts, runs game days, summarises incidents, owns recurring loops within bounded permissions. Far

The proposal's mental model can be stated plainly: the engineer is the author; the AI is a faster typewriter governed by templates. An AI-native model inverts that default: the agent is the author of routine artifacts; the engineer is the editor in chief, the architect, and the escalation point.

Honest summary. Wellhub is aligned on the discipline side of AI-native engineering (governance, artifacts, audit, neutrality). It is off on every dimension that actually distinguishes AI-native work from AI-assisted work (agents, evaluation, tool layer, autonomy gradient, in-flow surfaces, outcome measurement). The proposal raises the floor; the work ahead is to raise the ceiling.

4.A picture of the AI-native version

To make the gap concrete, here is what the same SDLC stages, the same Norms, and the same Safeguards would look like if reached differently. None of what follows requires discarding the current proposal. Most of it requires inverting one assumption: that the engineer is always the author. Once the agent is allowed to initiate, the rest of the architecture rearranges itself naturally.

Agents that do real work

  • PR-review agent. Runs on every pull request before a human looks at it. Applies the DB-migration checklist, the public-endpoint checklist, the observability-coverage check, and the Norms catalog. Human reviewers see a pre-triaged PR with the security and privacy boxes already verified or flagged.
  • Alert-coverage agent. Lives continuously against the canonical monitor catalog, opens PRs for gaps, owns the conversation on those PRs, and closes itself out. Today this is cia alert-coverage, a script a human must remember to invoke.
  • Post-deploy agent. Watches Argo, Datadog, and Grafana for the one-hour window automatically, summarises Golden Signals, escalates only when something looks off. Today this is cia post-deploy, also a script a human must remember to invoke.
  • On-call triage agent. Reads PagerDuty, Datadog, and Loki at incident start, drafts the timeline, proposes the rollback, links the relevant runbook. Today the runbook is a markdown template; the live incident work remains entirely manual.
  • Dependency agent. Classifies Dependabot risk, opens curated PRs, runs the test plan, escalates only the ambiguous ones.
  • IDE agent. Knows the REASONS Canvas, the Norms, and the Safeguards by default. The artifact discipline shows up where engineers already work, rather than in a CLI they have to remember.

The platform beneath the agents

  • A shared MCP tool layer exposing the gympass-app catalog, Datadog, Argo, Vault, IAM, Sonarcloud, and the Production Queries sheet as typed tools that any agent can call.
  • An evaluation flywheel providing golden datasets per agent, regression CI on prompt and tool changes, quality dashboards visible to engineering leadership, and a feedback loop from real PRs and incidents back into prompts and eval sets.
  • An agent governance model that codifies the autonomy gradient (copilot, autopilot, autonomous) per agent, with explicit permissions, blast-radius limits, audit logging, and rollback policy. This is the artefact Infosec, Privacy, and Fraud will need in order to approve scaled agentic engineering.

5.Agentic readiness today, and the real blockers

Based purely on what the document reveals, the position can be stated cleanly:

  • Strongest asset: artifact discipline. Prompts in git, two-way sync, central Norms and Safeguards. Reusable across whatever Wellhub builds next.
  • Biggest gap: there is no platform on which agents can plan, act, and self-correct, and no evaluation infrastructure that would tell anyone whether they are working.
  • Current stage: pre-agentic. The proposed system does not yet contain agents in any meaningful sense.

The blockers to moving along the agentic curve are organisational and platform-level, not model-level. Each is addressable in months rather than years, provided it is named explicitly:

  1. No agent runtime or orchestration substrate. A CLI invoked by a human is not a substrate. Agents need a place to hold state across steps, call tools, retry, escalate, and be observed.
  2. No evaluation infrastructure. Autonomy cannot scale beyond what can be measured. Without per-task eval sets and regression suites, no one will sign off on more autonomy.
  3. No shared tool layer. Today's integrations are scripts wired into one binary. Agents need a typed, shared tool surface (MCP or equivalent).
  4. No agent governance model. Before any agent acts in production, leadership needs explicit answers on which agents can merge, open PRs to which paths, touch which environments, hold which secrets, and how rollback works if an agent misbehaves.
  5. A cultural default that the human is the author. Every command in the proposal is shaped around an engineer invoking it. Moving to AI-native requires inverting that default.
  6. A single, narrow surface area. The CLI is one channel. Agents need to live where work happens: IDE, PR, on-call, dashboards, internal chat.

6.The path forward: five high-leverage moves

Five recommended moves, sequenced so that each is independently valuable and each strengthens the next. They are designed to be defensible at leadership level: every move is tied to a measurable outcome.

6.1 Reframe the bet around outcomes, not artifacts

Replace the current narrative ("we will add a CLI with N commands tied to SDLC stages") with a measurable outcome contract:

  • Reduce PR cycle time by X% in Y months on a pilot scope.
  • Reduce change failure rate by X% via agentic pre-review.
  • Reduce on-call human minutes per incident by X% via agentic triage.
  • Hold security and privacy gate pass-rate at 100% while the numbers above improve.

This shifts the conversation from "new tool" to "new operating leverage," and makes the programme falsifiable in a way leadership can evaluate.

6.2 Move from CLI to in-IDE and in-PR agents, keeping the artifact discipline

The strongest idea in the proposal (prompts as versioned artifacts) does not require a CLI. It requires shared, governed context. Concretely:

  • Adopt an in-editor agent with org-level rules loaded as system context. The REASONS Canvas, Norms, and Safeguards live there, not in templates/.
  • Add an agentic PR reviewer that runs on every PR before a human looks at it, applying the security checklist, DB-migration checklist, public-endpoint checklist, and observability coverage check.
  • Keep cia verify as the CI gate, but evolve it to verify agent output, not only CLI output.

Engineers do not have to change their workflow; the governance comes to them.

6.3 Build the evaluation flywheel before scaling autonomy

Stand up a small, sharp evaluation capability: golden datasets per use case, regression CI for any prompt or system-context change, quality dashboards visible to engineering leadership, and a feedback ingestion path from real PRs and incidents. Without this, no agent will ever earn more autonomy than it has on day one, and leadership will not sign off on giving it more. This is the single highest-leverage missing capability in the current design.

6.4 Pilot one fully autonomous agent on a high-volume, low-blast-radius loop

Pick one workflow Wellhub already runs manually, knows well, and can measure. The proposal already contains strong candidates:

  • Alert-coverage gap-filler. The document already proposes diffing current alerts against the canonical catalog. Let an agent open the PRs, with a human approving merges.
  • Dependency upgrade triage. Agent reads Dependabot output, classifies risk, opens curated PRs, drafts test plans.
  • On-call summariser. Agent reads PagerDuty, Datadog, and Loki at incident start, drafts the timeline, proposes the rollback. The human still calls the shot.

One credible autonomous agent in production is worth ten slide decks. It also generates the operational learning required to expand autonomy safely.

6.5 Stand up the MCP tool layer and an agent governance model in parallel

These are the platform investments that unlock everything beyond a pilot:

  • Tool layer. Expose Wellhub's internal systems (gympass-app catalog, Datadog, Argo, Vault, IAM, Sonarcloud, Production Queries sheet) as typed tools any agent can call. The current script orchestration becomes one consumer of that layer rather than the only one.
  • Governance model. Codify the autonomy gradient (copilot, autopilot, autonomous) per agent, with explicit permissions, blast-radius limits, audit logging, and rollback policy. This is the artefact Infosec, Privacy, and Fraud will need in order to approve scaled agentic engineering.

7.Closing perspective

SPDD/cia as currently designed will make Wellhub better governed. It will not, on its own, make Wellhub measurably faster, and it will not make Wellhub AI-native. Used as the whole strategy, it carries a real risk of becoming an enhanced governance layer rather than a step-change in delivery capability: more required artifacts, more required reviews, the same human bottleneck, with a stronger audit trail.

Used as the foundation for an agentic platform, however, the proposal becomes the first credible chapter of an AI-native transformation. The artifact discipline stays. An evaluation flywheel and a tool layer get built. The authorship default inverts. Specific workflows move along an explicit autonomy gradient. Each step is measurable, and each one keeps the human approvals the SDLC was designed to protect.

A twelve-month version of the story that leadership should be able to tell:

  • We standardised the context. Norms, Safeguards, and the REASONS Canvas are how every Wellhub agent reasons about our systems.
  • We built the substrate. A tool layer, an evaluation flywheel, and an agent governance model that Infosec, Privacy, and Fraud approved.
  • We moved load onto agents. In PR review, in alert coverage, in on-call triage, in dependency hygiene. The numbers moved with them (cycle time, change failure rate, MTTR, on-call hours).
  • We did not lose a gate. Every regulated stage of the SDLC kept its human approval. AI raised the floor; humans still own the ceiling.

That is the difference between an AI-assisted Wellhub and an AI-native Wellhub. The current proposal is the right first chapter. The work ahead is to write the rest of the book.
